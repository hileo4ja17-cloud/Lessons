a lesson in real-world English

Two ideas, one sentence.
That's the whole trick.

Forget "defining vs non-defining." In real life, English speakers join two ideas about the same person, place, thing or moment with one small word: who, which, that, where, when, whose. That's the whole grammar. Today we'll meet it in films, dating profiles, reviews and your own stories.

01

The simple idea

no grammar terms needed →

If you ever want to say two short sentences about the same thing, you can glue them with a tiny connector word.

I have a friend.
She works from home.
→
I have a friend who works from home.
This is the café.
We met there last summer.
→
This is the café where we met last summer.
I bought a jacket.
It was well made.
→
I bought a jacket that was well made.
I love that moment.
The hero finally smiles then.
→
I love the moment when the hero finally smiles.
My neighbour is kind.
Her dog walks itself, basically.
→
My neighbour, whose dog walks itself, is very kind.
02

Six connectors. That's it.

save this as your cheat sheet
who
about a person
The architect who designed our office is only 28.
which
about a thing / animal
The job, which pays really well, is fully remote.
that
person or thing — most common in speech
Customers that feel disappointed will leave a review.
where
about a place
I want a job where I can work from home.
when
about a time / moment
It was the moment when I felt truly charmed.
whose
when something belongs to someone
He's a writer whose books made me cry.
✦

The comma question? Stop overthinking it.

When you speak, commas don't matter — just say what feels natural. When you write, a quick rule: if the part feels like a quick side-note you could remove, add commas around it. If removing it would confuse the listener — skip the commas. That's the entire rule. Now let's move on.

09

Glue them together

two sentences become one
7
Combine each pair into a single sentence with a relative clause.
Type your answer, then hit check. Multiple correct answers are usually possible — the model answer is just one option.
1. about a writer
She's a writer. Her novels have won three awards.
Model: She's a writer whose novels have won three awards.
2. about a tour guide
I met a tour guide. He could speak five languages.
Model: I met a tour guide who could speak five languages.
3. about a café
We found a café. It serves the best matcha in town.
Model: We found a café that serves the best matcha in town.
4. about a day
I'll never forget that day. I joined this team that day.
Model: I'll never forget the day when I joined this team.
5. about a boss
My boss is great. She makes you feel encouraged on a Monday.
Model: My boss is the kind of person who makes you feel encouraged on a Monday.
6. about a film
The film left me a bit disappointed. Everyone recommended it.
Model: The film, which everyone recommended, left me a bit disappointed.

You just used relative clauses
like a native speaker.

You don't have to label them. You don't have to remember which kind is which. You just have to notice when you want to say two things about one thing — and reach for who, which, that, where, when, whose. That's the whole skill. The rest is practice and a little reading.
