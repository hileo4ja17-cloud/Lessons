2Presentation · Grammar≈ 20 min

The words that order time

We use time sequencers to say when or in what order things happen. Tap each card to see an example.

A story on a timeline

The same morning, told in order:

Watch out · key rules
✓
When joins two actions: “I was watching TV when the phone rang.” (two verbs in one sentence)
✓
For two actions one after another, use then or after that:
“I got up and got dressed. Then / After that, I made a cup of coffee.”
✕
Don't use after alone to mean "next":
“After I made a cup of coffee.” (when you mean then)
✓
Later and … minutes/days later show time passing:
“Two minutes later, my phone rang.” · “The next day, I went to work.”

Think and answer:
• In "When I arrived, she was cooking" — how many actions are there?
• Can I say "After, I went home" to mean "next"?
• Is "the next day" the same day or a different day?

2Presentation · Connectors≈ 12 min

because, so, but, although

Sequencers tell us when. Connectors tell us why and show contrast. Tap each card.

How they work
↺
because gives a reason; so gives a result. They are opposite directions:
“She drove fast because she was late.” = “She was late, so she drove fast.”
⇄
but and although both show a contrast:
“She was tired, but she couldn't sleep.” = “Although she was tired, she couldn't sleep.”
↕
although can go at the beginning or in the middle of the sentence:
“Although it was cold, …” · “…, although it was cold.”

Think and answer:
• In "I was hungry, so I ate" — which part is the reason, which is the result?
• What's the difference between because and so?
• Can although start a sentence? Can but?
• Do but and although show a similar idea or an opposite one?
