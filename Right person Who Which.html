Grammar · The Rule

Relative clauses
with who, which and that.

Three little words that quietly do enormous work — they tell us which person we mean, which thing we mean, and stitch two sentences into one elegant line.

5A
The grammar, unpacked.

Use relative clauses to give more information about people and things. They also let you combine two statements into one.

I want a job. I want it to be more interesting than this one.

= I want a job which is more interesting than this one.

Use which for things.

This is the machine which we use to make jeans.

Use who for people.

We're looking for someone who likes travelling.

You can use that instead of which and who.

This is the computer that I use for work.

He's the guy that got the job.

When the relative pronoun describes the object of the sentence, it can be omitted.

John's the person. I told you about him.

= John's the person (who) I told you about.

That's the camera. I want it.

= That's the camera (which) I want.
